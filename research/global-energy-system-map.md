# Global Energy System Map — How Risk Flows Through Energy Markets

The global energy system is not a collection of isolated markets.

It is a **connected network of supply chains, infrastructure, geopolitics, and financial signals**.

This page maps how **energy risk originates, propagates, and impacts markets**.

---

## The Big Picture

Energy risk flows through a structured system:

**Geopolitics → Supply → Infrastructure → Markets → Prices**

Understanding this chain is critical for:

- Energy traders  
- Macro investors  
- Analysts  
- Policy makers  

---

## 1. Geopolitical Layer (Origin of Risk)

Risk begins with geopolitical events:

- War and conflict  
- Sanctions and trade restrictions  
- Political instability  
- Strategic resource competition  

These events create **initial risk signals**.

## 2. Supply Layer (Physical Impact)

Geopolitical risk translates into:

- Oil supply disruptions  
- Gas flow constraints  
- LNG availability shifts  
- Production uncertainty  

This is where **risk becomes physical reality**.

---

## 3. Infrastructure Layer (Transmission Channels)

Energy flows through critical infrastructure:

- Pipelines  
- LNG terminals  
- Shipping routes  
- Storage systems  

Key vulnerabilities include:

- Chokepoints  
- Capacity limits  
- Transit risks

## 4. Market Layer (Amplification)

Markets interpret and amplify risk through:

- Price volatility (Brent, TTF)  
- Futures curves  
- Options and hedging flows  
- Speculative positioning  

At this stage, **risk becomes financial signal**.

## How the System Connects

The energy system is dynamic:

- Geopolitics drives supply risk  
- Supply risk stresses infrastructure  
- Infrastructure constraints amplify market reactions  
- Markets price expectations  
- Risk indices track underlying conditions  

This creates feedback loops.

---

## Key Insight

Energy markets do not move in isolation.

They respond to a **network of interdependent risks**.

Understanding the system requires:

→ Tracking multiple layers simultaneously  
→ Separating noise from structural signals  
→ Using both data and risk frameworks
